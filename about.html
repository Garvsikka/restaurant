The Beginning

Born From a Farmers' Market Conversation

Every restaurant has a founding moment. Ours happened at the Wychwood Barns market on a cold November Saturday.

Chef and co-founder Isabelle Moreau had spent a decade in Toronto's fine dining scene — technically accomplished, internationally inspired, and quietly dissatisfied. She kept returning to the same question: why were Ontario restaurants importing flavour when the province grew some of the finest ingredients in North America?

At the market that November, she met Thomas Andrade, a third-generation vegetable farmer from Prince Edward County who'd been struggling to find restaurant partners willing to commit to his seasonal schedule. They talked for three hours. The Harvest Table was the result.

A Short History

Six Years, One Commitment

Spring 2018

We Open Our Doors

The Harvest Table opens on Elmwood Avenue with 42 covers, a hand-written menu printed daily, and a 90-day supply agreement with five Ontario farms. We sell out every service for the first month.

Summer 2019

The Wine List Takes Shape

After a week-long trip through the Niagara Peninsula visiting VQA producers, our sommelier Laura Tsang builds the all-Ontario wine programme that would become one of our most-discussed features. We now partner with eleven Niagara wineries.

Winter 2020

We Expand to 70 Covers

A gentle renovation doubles our capacity while preserving the intimate atmosphere. We open a small private dining room — The Cellar — which seats twelve and has become Toronto's quietest, most-requested dinner party secret.

Summer 2022

Toronto Life Best New Restaurants List

Toronto Life names us among the city's essential restaurants. We're described as "the farm-to-table experience that actually means it." We frame the review and hang it in the kitchen where the cooks can see it.

Today

Rooted, and Still Growing

We partner with sixteen Ontario farms, six VQA wineries, and four Ontario Craft Brewers members. The menu changes every six to eight weeks. The butter tart cheesecake has never left.

In the Kitchen

Chef Isabelle Moreau

A Toronto-born cook who found her voice in Ontario's fields rather than European kitchens.

Before The Harvest Table, Isabelle trained at George Brown College, staged in Montréal and Lyon, and spent four years as sous-chef at one of Toronto's most celebrated fine dining establishments. She left not because she failed — she left because she had a better idea.

"Ontario gives me everything I need," she says. "I don't need to import drama. The drama is in a first-of-season Prince Edward County asparagus, or a Niagara Peninsula Gamay poured alongside it. My job is to not get in the way."

Isabelle was named a Globe and Mail Future Food Leader in 2023 and sits on the board of the Ontario Culinary Tourism Alliance.

From the Ground Up

The Farms Behind Our Menu

We name our partners because they deserve the recognition.

Prince Edward County

Andrade Family Farm

Third-generation vegetable growers specialising in heirloom varieties. Thomas Andrade's French breakfast radishes and heritage squash appear on our menu from May through November.

Grey County

Hillcrest Heritage Farm

Pasture-raised heritage-breed pigs, chickens, and beef cattle raised without hormones or antibiotics. The source of our beloved short rib and our roasted chicken programme.

Niagara Peninsula

Blossom Creek Orchards

A family orchard growing 40+ varieties of Ontario apples, stone fruits, and berries on the Beamsville Bench. Their Spy apples are the backbone of our autumn galette.

Prince Edward County

Sandbanks Creamery

Small-batch artisan cheeses made from milk sourced within ten kilometres of the dairy. Their aged cheddar and soft chèvre are permanent fixtures on our cheese board.

Grey County

Beaver River Honey Co.

Wildflower and buckwheat honey harvested from hives across the Grey Highlands. Their buckwheat honey appears in our desserts and is used to sweeten house cocktails.

Georgian Bay

North Shore Fisheries

Sustainably caught Lake Erie pickerel, Georgian Bay whitefish, and Lake Huron perch. The freshest fish in Toronto arrives here first — usually within 24 hours of the catch.

Our Commitment

Seasonality Isn't a Marketing Word. It's Our Entire Kitchen.

When you eat here in June, you're eating what Ontario grows in June. Period.

We don't carry a year-round tomato. We don't import asparagus from Peru in February to satisfy a habit. We let the province dictate the menu, and we find that constraint forces us to be far more creative — and far more delicious — than we'd be with full global supply.

In winter, that means root vegetables, preserved summer harvests, braised cuts, and aged cheeses. In summer, it means abundance — and the pressure to do justice to the short, fleeting window when Ontario produce is at its most extraordinary.
